Exercise 4b - Impedance Ratio Effects

Introduction

Introduction

Background

During wave propagation the presence of different interfaces will affect the amplitudes of the wave travel. The partitioning of energy at the interface is a function of the specific impedance of materials in the different media. The specific impedance is a measure of the density and wave propagation velocity in a particular material.

 BACK TO TOP

Objective

After this exercise you should be able to show how the impedance ratio affects the proportion of the wave that is trapped on soft layers and the effect of a transmitting boundary on the dissipation of energy in an semi-infinite soil layer.

 BACK TO TOP

Things to Do

  1. Open the Dr. Layer program. Starting with the initial configuration, assign to the bottom eight layers a "very fast" wave velocity and to the upper four layers a "slow" wave velocity as shown in Figure 1.

  2. Select a "pulse" load condition and assign to it a high amplitude and high frequency (close to 80% of max). Select from the "options" menu the "Release Base" and "Radiation Damping" conditions.



  3. Using the results from exercises 1 (basic concepts) and 3 (impedance ratio) determine the specific impedance of each layer group and the impedance ratio at the interface between the two materials.

  4. Starting with the set up described above, move the cursor to the upper left portion of the screen and press on the time increment button to set up the wave motion. You will immediately notice a pulse wave that travels in the upward direction.

  5. At the interface part of the energy carried by the wave will be transmitted and part will be reflected. Note the direction and amplitude of the transmitted and reflected waves.

  6. The reflected portion of the wave will travel down through the lower material and once it reaches the base it will be completely absorbed. Note that at this point the wave amplitude completely vanishes. To simulate this phenomenon DrLayer uses a transmitting element characterized by a simple dashpot connected to the base. This condition allow us simulate a realistic semiinfinite soil layer.

  7. The transmitted portion of the wave will travel through the upper layer with a slower wave speed and eventually will reach a free boundary. Based on the results from exercise 2 we can now ascertain that at this boundary the wave will be completely reflected.

  8. The reflected wave will travel down through material 2 and eventually will reach again the interface between the two materials. At this point part of the wave will be transmitted and part will be reflected.

  9. As time goes on these interactions will repeat several times. Observe the effect of time on the amplitude of the trapped wave.

Observation

Notice that during the motion a fraction of the energy carried by the wave is entertained (or trapped) in the upper layer. This fraction diminishes as time goes on and eventually the system reaches the at rest condition. Base on your current knowledge on wave propagation explain why the motion dissipates??.
